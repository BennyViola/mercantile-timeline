Defense Playbook

Access Defense

If they say: “We needed the reset because we lacked control of the network.”

Response Best reply
You say: The record says the opposite. DM requested shared access in January, confirmed users were active, and by early February BH documented DM had “full unlimited control” except deleting BH accounts. The March 8 reset looks like exclusion, not access recovery.

Use timeline cards: Jan 15 request for “shared visibility,” Feb 2 “full unlimited control,” Mar 8 reset “to remove BH access.”

Event Support Defense

If they say: “BH wasn’t in a position to support the March 12 event anyway.”

Response First refusal
You say: The point is not just capacity; it is contractual sequence. BH was never asked to support the event, and Andy’s narrative treats that as a direct violation of the SPA’s first right‑of‑refusal while DM was simultaneously using copied files and substitute vendors.

Use cards: Mar 10–12 copying sequence and the statement that BH “was never asked to support this event.”

IP Defense

If they say: “We didn’t steal IP; we made new files.”

Response
You say: Making new files after copying the old ones does not erase the copying. The timeline records copied project files, testing and reverse engineering, “getting into” BH templates, and Michael’s own statement: “We copied your file… and then we created new.”

Use cards: NDA baseline, Mar 10 hard‑drive transfer, Mar 10 reverse engineering, Mar 12 copied‑file admission.

Money Defense

If they say: “Blockhouse’s numbers are inflated or speculative.”

Response
You say: Separate the buckets. There is a hard‑cost floor that is not speculative, and there is a second category for the partnership value and future upside that DM itself sold to BH through conservative pro formas, revenue sharing, and the seed‑enterprise pitch.

Use cards: Aug 2 “germinating entertainment enterprise,” Oct 9 “very conservative” pro formas, dealer‑rate / millions framing, and your damages‑bucket slide.

Motive / Investor Defense

If they say: “There’s no proof we used BH to help secure venue value or investors.”

Response
You say: The record supports at minimum that DM sold BH on a long‑term investor‑facing partnership model, tied BH’s content and AV role to venue success, and later used BH’s work while trying to separate BH from the upside. You do not need to overclaim to make that point land.

Use cards: Aug 2 investor / content‑partner story, late‑2024 sales framing, and the high‑level case theory tile on BH brand / IP helping secure Mercantile and Phoenix.

One‑Off Vendor Defense

If they say: “This was just a vendor relationship, not some bigger partnership.”

Response
You say: Their own executives described it as larger than one venue, a “germinating entertainment enterprise,” with a real content partner that would be paid up front and continue to benefit as the venue and content became more successful. That is not ordinary vendor language.

Use cards: Aug 2 seed‑enterprise framing, Oct 11 SPA signing under pressure, and the sales / investor narrative around Mercantile and Phoenix.

Performance Defense

If they say: “BH wasn’t delivering, so we had to bring in others.”
